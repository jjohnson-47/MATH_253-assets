Determinants, Cofactor Expansion & Geometry

Goal: See how a simple formula like ad - bc in 2‑D grows into powerful tools for 3‑D geometry, the cross product, and multivariable integration.

1. 2×2 Determinant = Signed Area Scale Factor

Given matrix $$A = \begin{bmatrix}a & b\\ c & d\end{bmatrix},$$ its determinant is $$\det A = ad - bc.$$

  • The columns of $A$ can be treated as vectors u and v.
  • The parallelogram they span has area $|ad - bc|$.
  • Multiplying any vector by $A$ “scales & shears” the plane. $\det A$ tells how signed area changes: a scale factor (< 0 means orientation flips).

2. 3×3 Determinant = Signed Volume

For $$B = \begin{bmatrix}a_1 & b_1 & c_1\\ a_2 & b_2 & c_2\\ a_3 & b_3 & c_3\end{bmatrix},$$ $\det B$ gives the signed volume of the parallelepiped spanned by its column vectors.

Cross‑Product Shortcut

The cross product formula is neatly packaged as a determinant with the unit vectors in the top row (a handy mnemonic):

$$\vec u \times \vec v = \begin{vmatrix} \hat\imath & \hat\jmath & \hat k \\ u_1 & u_2 & u_3 \\ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{vmatrix}.$$

3. Cofactor Expansion (Laplace Expansion)

To compute $\det B$ without row‑reducing, expand along any row or column:

$$\det B = \sum_{j=1}^{3} (-1)^{i+j}\, b_{ij}\,M_{ij},$$ where $M_{ij}$ is the minor (the 2×2 determinant left after deleting row i and column j).

Why useful? • Lets you pick a row/column with zeros for quick work. • Generalizes to any size matrix.

4. Determinants in Multivariable Integration (Jacobian)

In change‑of‑variables, the determinant of the Jacobian matrix measures how small volume elements stretch.

Polar Coordinates in 2‑D: $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$  →  Jacobian

$$J = \begin{vmatrix}\frac{\partial x}{\partial r} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}\\[4pt] \frac{\partial y}{\partial r} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial \theta}\end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \phantom{-}r\cos\theta \end{vmatrix}= r(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta)=r.$$

So an infinitesimal area $dA$ in the plane becomes $J\,dr\,d\theta = r\,dr\,d\theta$.

Concrete Example

Compute the area of the unit disk $x^2 + y^2 \le 1$:

\[ A = \iint_{\text{Disk}} \! dA = \int_{0}^{2\pi}\!\int_{0}^{1} r\,dr\,d\theta = \int_{0}^{2\pi}\!\frac{1}{2}d\theta = \pi. \]

The Jacobian determinant r makes the integral “just work.” In higher‑dimensional calculus, the same idea carries through with 3×3 Jacobians for cylindrical, spherical, or more exotic coordinate systems.

5. Key Takeaways

  • Geometry first: determinants measure signed area/volume scaling.
  • Cofactor expansion is a systematic way to build determinants from 2×2 pieces.
  • Cross product leverages a 3×3 determinant for a tidy formula.
  • Jacobian determinants turn up whenever we warp coordinates in multiple integrals.
