Standard Form Equation

A plane can be described by a linear equation involving all three variables:

$$ax + by + cz = d$$

Where $\langle a, b, c \rangle$ is the normal vector to the plane, perpendicular to every vector that lies in the plane.

Normal Vector Form

Given a normal vector $\vec{n} = \langle a, b, c \rangle$ and a point $P_0(x_0, y_0, z_0)$ on the plane:

$$a(x - x_0) + b(y - y_0) + c(z - z_0) = 0$$

This form directly shows how the normal vector determines the plane's orientation.

Three-Point Form

Given three non-collinear points $P_1$, $P_2$, and $P_3$, the normal vector is:

$$\vec{n} = \overrightarrow{P_1P_2} \times \overrightarrow{P_1P_3}$$

The cross product of two vectors in the plane gives a vector perpendicular to the plane.

Parallel and Perpendicular Planes

Two planes are:

  • Parallel if their normal vectors are parallel: $\vec{n_1} = k\vec{n_2}$
  • Perpendicular if their normal vectors are perpendicular: $\vec{n_1} \cdot \vec{n_2} = 0$

The angle between planes equals the angle between their normal vectors.

üìê Essential Formulas

Distance from Point to Plane

$$d = \frac{|ax_0 + by_0 + cz_0 + d|}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2 + c^2}}$$

Where $(x_0, y_0, z_0)$ is the external point and $ax + by + cz + d = 0$ is the plane equation.

Distance between Parallel Planes

$$d = \frac{|d_1 - d_2|}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2 + c^2}}$$

For parallel planes $ax + by + cz + d_1 = 0$ and $ax + by + cz + d_2 = 0$.

Angle between Planes

$$\cos \theta = \frac{|\vec{n_1} \cdot \vec{n_2}|}{|\vec{n_1}||\vec{n_2}|}$$

The acute angle between two planes with normal vectors $\vec{n_1}$ and $\vec{n_2}$.

üîÑ Lines vs Planes Comparison

Lines in 3D Space

  • Dimension: 1D objects in 3D space
  • Direction: Defined by direction vector
  • Equation: Parametric form $\vec{r}(t) = \vec{r_0} + t\vec{v}$
  • Degrees of Freedom: 1 parameter
  • Intersection: Point, line, or empty

Planes in 3D Space

  • Dimension: 2D surfaces in 3D space
  • Orientation: Defined by normal vector
  • Equation: Linear form $ax + by + cz = d$
  • Degrees of Freedom: 2 parameters
  • Intersection: Point, line, plane, or empty

üåç Real-World Applications

Architecture & Construction

Building floors, walls, and roofs are modeled as planes. Engineers calculate intersections for structural joints and determine optimal angles for stability.

Computer Graphics

3D rendering engines use plane equations for surface normal calculations, lighting effects, and collision detection in virtual environments.

Geology & Mining

Rock layers and fault planes are modeled mathematically to predict mineral deposits and assess geological stability for construction projects.

Crystallography

Crystal faces are described by plane equations, helping scientists understand molecular structures and predict material properties.

Aerospace Engineering

Wing surfaces, control surfaces, and fuselage panels are modeled as planes for aerodynamic analysis and stress calculations.

üìñ Example 11.6.11: Plane-Line Intersection

Medical Imaging

CT and MRI scans create cross-sectional images by intersecting the body with mathematical planes at different angles and positions.
